ALCOHOL CONSUMPTION AROUND THE WORLD

This dashboard explores the level of alcohol consumption by people of various countries and continents around the world and the various related factors. This helps readers, and specifically those who consume alcohol or are interested in the domain, in understanding a multitude of factors which highlight certain trends one sees in their own drinking patterns or those around them.

We will first obtain an overview of relative consumption between different countries through the use of a choropleth map where the Pure Alcohol in Litres has been normalised based on each country's average.

Next, we will gauge how the different factors (explained on the right) are related using a correlation plot. Interesting patterns observed will be further explored as we move down the dashboard.

The factors explored include:

  • Country
  • Continent
  • Income per Person: GDP per capita in constant US$.
  • Alcohol Comsumption: Per capita consumption in litres pure alcohol.
  • Suicide per 100th: Suicide per standard population 100,000 Mortality
  • Employment Rate: Employees age 15+ as a percentage of total population.
  • Urban Rate: Urban population as a percentage of total population.
  • Happiness Score: Average happiness rating out of 10.
  • Beer Servings: Average annual serving sizes of beer per person.
  • Wine Servings: Average annual serving sizes of wine per person.
  • Spirit Servings: Average annual serving sizes of spirit per person.

How Does Alcohol Consumption Differ in Different Countries?

In the above map, higher consumption is highlighted through darker colours, in which two specific countries, Belarus and Poland stand out as having the greatest consumption.

In contrast, countries where alcohol is illegal (dry countries), seen in areas of Asia and Africa, are also highlighted in a pale blue colour which also brings to attention contrasting viewpoints amongst neighbouring countries.

Greatest consumers in each continent:

  • Europe consumes the most alcohol, on average, with Belarus consuming the greatest global average of 14.2L.
  • North America: Grenada with 11.9L.
  • South America: Argentina with 8.3L.
  • Africa: Nigeria with 9.1L.
  • Asia: South Korea with 9.8L.
  • Oceania: Australia with 10.4L.

Which Factors are Strongly Related to Alcohol Consumption?

Looking at the correlations holistically, the strongest correlation is between Alcohol (Litres) and Beer Servings, with a correlation of 0.84 which highlights that beer is a popular beverage among countries with higher consumption rates, followed by spirit servings and then wine servings. Other than this, there is no other significant correlations when taking all continents into consideration.
To note which beverage is most popular based on each continent, we can look at the relative correlations between Alcohol (Litres) and Beer, Spirit, and Wine Servings. These relative differences are emphasied in the chart below.

Interesting correlations to note in Continents:

  • North America, South America, Africa and Oceania all have a strong positive relationship between Income and Wine servings with correlations between 0.59 - 0.94.
  • North America has a strong positive between happiness score and both beer and wine servings, but a slight negative correlation with happiness score and spirit servings.

  • South America has a strong negative relationship between beer servings and suicide.
  • Europe is the only continent with a negative relationship between Alcohol (Litres) and Income.
  • Oceania also has a strong correlation between Income and Beer Servings.

What is the Relationship between Alcohol Consumption and Happiness Levels?

Looking at the heatmap holistically (taking all continents into consideration), a strong positive trend is observed. An increase in pure alcohol is correlated with an increase in the average happiness score.

There are no countries with an average happiness score < 4 and average alcohol consumption > 6L, meaning that there are no countries with a combination of a low happiness score and relatively high alcohol consumption. HOWEVER, there are also no countries with an average happiness score > 6 and averagge alcohol consumption > 12L, meaning that an excess of alchol is also not good. A similar pattern is observed in North America, South America and Europe.

Overall, the happiest countries are those that drink between 8-10L while the unnhappiest countries are those that drink between 0-6L.

What is the Relationship between Alcohol Consumption and Sucicide?

In the above plot it is evident that there is a strong positive relationship between suicide and alcohol consumption. This relationship is particularly prevalent in Europe, Oceania, and Asia. Africa has a sligght positive relationship but mostly plateaus out at higher alcohol consumption.

On the other hand, North America has a cluster towards the bottom centre of the chart and does not show any significant trend. The same is observed in North America with the exception of two outliers, Suriname, and Guyana which have a much higher suicide rate than the cluster of other South American countries.

Oceania has a cluster of countries towards the lower ennd of both suicide rate and alcohol consumptionn with the exception of: New Zealand, Australia and Palau which still have a relatively losw suicide rate but a higher alcohol consumption.

What is interesting to note is that there is a general positive correlation between alcohol consumption and suicide rate but also a positive correlation between alcohol consumption and happiness scores (but to a ceratin extent).
